Unit 1 · Measurement

How chemists count and how careful they are

Chemistry is the study of stuff (matter) and how it changes. Before any of that, you need a way to measure stuff honestly. This unit is about being honest with numbers — saying what you actually know and not pretending you know more.

1.0 The subfields of chemistry

Chemistry is one giant subject, but chemists split it into "departments" so each can specialize:

SubfieldWhat it studies
Organic chemistryCarbon-based compounds — fuels, plastics, food, DNA.
Inorganic chemistryEverything not based on carbon — metals, rocks, salts.
Physical chemistryThe "why" of chemistry — energy, speed, the physics behind reactions.
Thermochemistry (sub-branch of physical)Energy changes (heat in/out) tied to chemical and physical changes. Endothermic vs exothermic lives here.
Analytical chemistryThe detective — what's in this sample, and how much?
BiochemistryChemistry happening inside living things.
Nuclear chemistryChanges inside the nucleus — radioactivity, fission, fusion.
ElectrochemistryChemistry that involves electricity (batteries, electrolysis).
heads-up question "What's the subfield of physical chemistry focused on energy changes in chemical and physical processes?" → Thermochemistry. (It's a subset of physical chemistry, not its own top-level field.)

1.1 Significant figures — how honest your number is

in plain words Significant figures are the digits you actually trust. Writing extra digits is like lying about how good your ruler was.

Measure your height with a cheap ruler — you can say "170 cm," maybe "170.5 cm." You can't say "170.5283 cm" — the ruler never gave you that. Those last digits would be made up.

The four rules

RuleExampleWhy
Any non-zero digit counts457 → 3 sig figsYou measured those digits.
"Sandwich" zeros count4007 → 4 sig figsYou can't accidentally measure a zero in the middle.
Front (leading) zeros never count0.0034 → 2 sig figsJust placeholders for the decimal point.
Trailing zeros: only with a decimal point4500 → 2 · 4500. → 4Without the dot we can't tell measurement from rounding.

why the rules exist Every measurement is really saying "I'm sure up to here, and the last digit is my best guess." The number of sig figs is literally the count of digits you're confident in. The zero rules just keep you from claiming more confidence than you have.

Math with sig figs

  • Multiplying or dividing: answer gets the fewest sig figs of anything you used.
  • Adding or subtracting: answer gets the fewest decimal places (places after the dot, not total digits).
Worked example — mixed operations
(13.7 + 2.03) ÷ 0.250
Inside first (addition). 13.7 + 2.03 = 15.73. Round by decimal places: 13.7 has 1 → keep 1 → 15.7.
Divide. 15.7 has 3 sig figs; 0.250 has 3 → answer keeps 3.
15.7 ÷ 0.250 = 62.8.
62.8
common mistake Switching the two rules — using "fewest decimal places" for multiplication or counting total digits for addition. Ask first: am I adding or multiplying? Then pick the matching rule.

1.3 Accuracy vs precision — two words people mix up

in plain words Accuracy = close to the right answer. Precision = your tries are close to each other. Not the same thing!

Darts at a bullseye. All in the center = accurate AND precise. Tight cluster up in the corner = precise but inaccurate. Scattered but averaging to the center = lucky on accuracy, not precise.

why this matters in lab A consistently wrong instrument (a scale that always reads 2 g heavy) gives precise but inaccurate data — and that's dangerous because the data looks trustworthy. Precision is about repeatability; accuracy is about calibration to truth.

2.5 Bohr model — excite an electron, watch it glow

Electrons sit on fixed "steps." Pump in energy and one jumps up to a higher step. That excited state is unstable — it falls back down and releases the leftover energy as a photon. Bigger fall = higher-energy (bluer/violet) photon. Smaller fall = lower-energy (redder) photon.

3.2 The four trends — and the ONE idea behind them

All four trends come from one tug-of-war: nucleus pull (more protons = stronger) vs shielding/distance (inner electrons block; outer shells feel less).

Nucleus is a magnet, electrons are paperclips. More protons = stronger magnet; more shells in between = weaker pull on the outer clip. Every trend is this magnet game.

4.2 Naming compounds — quick rules

  1. Metal + nonmetal → "metal + nonmetal-ide" (NaCl = sodium chloride).
  2. Transition metal (variable charge) → add Roman numeral (FeCl₃ = iron(III) chloride).
  3. Polyatomic ion → use its name (Ca(NO₃)₂ = calcium nitrate).
  4. Two nonmetals → use prefixes mono-, di-, tri-, tetra- (CO₂ = carbon dioxide).
  5. Acids: no oxygen → hydro-…-ic; -ate → -ic acid; -ite → -ous acid.
criss-cross trick Write each ion's charge; swap each one down as the other's subscript. Al³⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → Al₂(SO₄)₃.

4.3 VSEPR — why molecules aren't flat blobs

in plain words Electron groups push each other away, so a molecule spreads its bonds and lone pairs as far apart as possible. That spreading sets the 3-D shape.

Tie balloons together at their knots. They naturally splay out to get as far from each other as they can. Electron groups do exactly this.

why lone pairs bend things Lone pairs push harder than bonded pairs. In water, oxygen has 4 groups (2 bonds + 2 lone) — basically tetrahedral, but we only "see" the 2 H's, giving a bent shape. The two pushy lone pairs squeeze the H–O–H angle from 109.5° down to ~104.5°.

4.4 Molecular polarity — bond dipoles add up

A bond is polar if the two atoms have different EN. A molecule is polar if the bond dipoles don't cancel by symmetry. CO₂ has two polar bonds, but they point opposite ways → cancel → nonpolar molecule. H₂O has bent shape → dipoles don't cancel → polar.

4.5 Intermolecular forces — the "stickiness"

Bonds hold atoms together inside a molecule. IMFs are the weaker attractions between separate molecules — what decides boiling point.

  1. London dispersion — present in everything; bigger molecules = stronger.
  2. Dipole–dipole — between polar molecules, lining up like tiny magnets.
  3. Hydrogen bonding — super-strong dipole, only when H is bonded to N, O, or F (FON).

≋ IMF identifier

Which strongest IMF holds the given liquid together?

why water is weird Water is small and light, should boil at very low temperature. But H-bonds grab neighbors so hard that it boils at 100 °C. This one fact explains ice floating, surface tension, and why life can exist.
like dissolves like Polar dissolves polar. Nonpolar dissolves nonpolar. Salt & sugar dissolve in water; oil doesn't. Answer to "why does/doesn't X dissolve?" is almost always "the IMFs match (or don't)."
Unit 5 · Chemical Reactions

Atoms rearrange into new combinations

Bonds break, bonds form, atoms swap partners. Recognize the pattern, balance the equation, track which electrons moved.

5.0 Endothermic vs exothermic — where does the heat live?

in plain words Every reaction either takes in heat from its surroundings (gets cold to the touch) or releases heat into its surroundings (gets warm to the touch).
  • Endothermic — absorbs heat. Heat acts like a reactant (it's needed as input). Container feels cold. Example: an instant cold pack, photosynthesis, melting ice.
  • Exothermic — releases heat. Heat acts like a product (it's given off). Container feels hot. Example: combustion, hand warmers, freezing water.
endothermic: reactants + heat → products  ·  exothermic: reactants → products + heat

5.2 Balancing — atoms are never created or destroyed

in plain words Same number of each kind of atom on both sides. Only change the big numbers in front (coefficients), never the little subscripts.
common mistake Changing a subscript "to make it balance." That makes a different substance. H₂O is water; H₂O₂ is hydrogen peroxide — totally different (and bleachy).

5.3 Redox — electrons changing hands

in plain words Redox = reduction–oxidation. Electrons move from one atom to another. OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

Use oxidation numbers to track who lost/gained electrons. Rules:

  • Pure element alone (O₂, Na) = 0.
  • Oxygen usually −2; hydrogen usually +1.
  • Charges in a compound must sum to 0 (or to ion's charge).

± Oxidation-number solver

Worked example — sulfur in H₂SO₄
2 hydrogens at +1 = +2. 4 oxygens at −2 = −8.
Let S = x. Compound is neutral → (+2) + x + (−8) = 0.
x − 6 = 0 → x = +6.
S = +6
Unit 6 · Stoichiometry

The math of "how much"

Once you can count atoms in giant bundles (moles) and read a recipe (equation), you can predict exactly how much product comes out of any starting amount.

6.1 The mole — chemistry's "dozen"

in plain words A mole is just a counting word for a huge bundle: 6.022 × 10²³ things. Atoms are tiny, so we count them in bundles.
  • 1 mol = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number).
  • 1 mol of a substance weighs its molar mass in grams.
  • 1 mol of any gas at STP takes up 22.4 L.

6.2 The mole map — every stoichiometry problem

the one rule You can never convert grams of A straight to grams of B. You must pass through moles in the middle. Grams → moles → (cross recipe ratio) → moles → grams.

Like exchanging dollars for yen — you can't compare directly, you go through a shared rate. In chemistry the shared rate is moles, and the recipe's coefficients are the exchange rate between substances.

6.3 Limiting reactant — what runs out first

in plain words The reactant you run out of first limits how much product you can make. The other one is in excess.

10 buns but only 4 patties → you make 4 burgers. The patties decide, the buns are leftovers. Doesn't matter how many extra buns you have.

Unit 7 · Acids & Bases

The H⁺ universe

Acids and bases are all about hydrogen ions (H⁺). One side donates them; the other side accepts them. Everything from sour taste to soap to body fluids is built from this trade.

7.1 What acids and bases are

  • Acid — donates H⁺ ("proton giver"). Sour, fizzes with metals.
  • Base — accepts H⁺ or produces OH⁻ ("proton taker"). Bitter, slippery.
DefinitionAcid is…Base is…
Arrheniusmakes H⁺ in watermakes OH⁻ in water
Brønsted–Lowryproton donorproton acceptor
Lewis (broadest)electron-pair acceptorelectron-pair donor
strong vs weak Strong = fully splits up in water. Weak = only partly splits up. "Strong" doesn't mean dangerous — it means "fully dissociates."

7.2 Concentration & dilution

Molarity = moles of solute ÷ liters of solution. Adding water doesn't change how much solute is in there — just the volume. So M₁V₁ = M₂V₂.

7.3 pH — the acid-strength scale

in plain words pH 0–14. Below 7 = acidic. 7 = neutral (water). Above 7 = basic. Each step = 10× change in [H⁺].
pH = −log[H⁺]  ·  pOH = −log[OH⁻]  ·  pH + pOH = 14

Unit 8 · Gases

Tiny particles, lots of empty space

A gas is just a bunch of fast little particles bouncing in a mostly-empty box. Almost every gas law follows from this one image.

8.1 KMT & the gas-law sandbox

  1. Particles fly fast in random directions.
  2. Their own size is negligible compared to the empty space.
  3. Collisions are perfect bounces — no energy lost.
  4. They don't attract or repel each other.
  5. Hotter gas = faster particles.
what pressure really is Particles banging on the walls. More banging, or harder banging, = more pressure. Squeeze → more collisions per second → higher P. Heat → faster, harder hits → higher P.
